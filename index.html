Transit Inequity in the San Francisco Bay Area

Visualizing access gaps and service disparities across income and geography

Overview

This project investigates transit access disparities across the Bay Area using spatial analysis and open data. Dashboards below show areas completely lacking access and zones with low-frequency transit service, visualized alongside demographic and income patterns.

Dashboard 1: No Transit Access Zones

This dashboard identifies areas outside a ¼-mile walkable buffer from any bus or rail stop. These locations are true “transit deserts” — communities with no formal access to the regional transit network.

Dashboard 2: Low-Frequency Transit Areas

This dashboard highlights communities with transit stops, but with low service frequency — typically fewer than 3 trips per hour. These areas represent hidden inaccessibility, where transit exists but is not reliable enough for daily use.

Methodology

Using ArcGIS Pro, we conducted overlay analysis to buffer transit stops, identify low-frequency services, and intersect results with demographic and income data from the 2020 ACS. Results were symbolized by severity and visualized in ArcGIS Dashboards.

Findings

Communities in East Bay (e.g., Richmond, Antioch, San Leandro) and outer suburban areas showed significant lack of access and low-frequency service. These areas also had higher proportions of lower-income households and communities of color, highlighting structural transit inequities.

Conclusion

Transit availability alone is not enough — frequency and walkable access are key to mobility equity. This work supports the need for regional investment in underserved neighborhoods and more transparent service planning tools.
