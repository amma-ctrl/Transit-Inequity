Transit Inequity in NYC & the Bay Area

Visualizing access gaps and service disparities across income and geography

Introduction & Research Design

Access to reliable public transportation is a foundational element of urban equity, shaping economic opportunity, social inclusion, and quality of life. This project explores whether federally funded public transit systems in two of the most transit-reliant cities in the U.S.—New York City and the San Francisco Bay Area—provide equitable service across income levels and geographies.

Research Questions:

  • Do lower-income communities have comparable access to public transit infrastructure as wealthier communities?
  • Where are the geographic “transit deserts” that lack even minimal access to federally funded transit services?
  • How does the frequency of transit service vary across income levels?

Hypotheses: Low-income census tracts have less access to frequent transit service than wealthier tracts, and areas with no transit access are disproportionately located in low- to moderate-income zones.

This research builds on findings by Manaugh & El-Geneidy (2012) and Karner & Niemeier (2013), who argue that frequency and reliability—beyond stop proximity—are critical indicators of transit equity.

Methodology

This study used ArcGIS Pro to prepare, analyze, and visualize transit and demographic data through a three-layer map structure embedded in a dashboard:

  • Transit Frequency: Using GTFS data, stops were flagged as “low-frequency” if they had average weekday headways of 20+ minutes.
  • Low-Frequency Stop Counts: Total low-frequency stops per census tract were calculated using spatial join, then visualized as semi-transparent orange circles.
  • No Access Zones: Red diamonds mark zones more than 0.5 miles from any bus/train stop and 0.75 miles from any ferry stop—areas considered to have no practical access to transit.
  • Demographic Overlay: Census tracts were color-coded by median household income using a yellow-green-blue scale across 5 brackets:
    • < $50,000
    • $50,000–$85,000
    • $85,000–$125,000
    • $125,000–$200,000
    • > $200,000

Findings

Low-Frequency Stops

In both NYC and the Bay Area, low-frequency stops cluster in outer boroughs or suburban peripheries. Queens, Staten Island, North San Jose, and Alameda County show notable concentrations—particularly in tracts earning under $85,000. This pattern reinforces the idea that transit frequency is unequally distributed.

No Access Zones

These transit deserts—areas completely outside effective transit reach—appear most often in outlying geographies: Staten Island’s eastern edge, Contra Costa County, and areas bordering the Bronx River. While some overlap with lower-income tracts, others affect middle-income zones as well.

Demographic Insights

High-frequency transit and dense service areas tend to align with higher-income tracts. Conversely, moderate- to low-income areas are disproportionately impacted by infrequent or nonexistent service—highlighting a clear equity gap.

Evaluation & Limitations

This project succeeds in making spatial transit inequities visible—layering frequency, access, and income to reveal service gaps that a simple coverage map might miss. However, several limitations should be noted:

  • Only weekday service was analyzed; off-peak or weekend frequencies may differ significantly.
  • GTFS headways were averaged, not weighted by ridership or time-of-day.
  • Federal funding levels per route or agency were not included in this version.

Despite these, the dashboard provides a strong foundation for advocacy and deeper research into equitable transit funding and design.

Next Steps

  • Incorporate actual funding data for each agency and route.
  • Add racial and car access data for sharper equity comparisons.
  • Apply this framework to other metro areas or run a historical comparison.

The long-term goal is to develop a national-level toolkit for evaluating federally funded transit through an equity lens—balancing frequency, access, and demographics.
